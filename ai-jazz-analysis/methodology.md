# Methodology

## 1. Six-Axis Jazz Evaluation Rubric

Each piece is rated 1–5 on six axes. Scores should be integers. Half-scores are not used; when in doubt, round down. Total score = sum of six axes (max 30).

---

### Axis 1: Harmonic Authenticity

*Does the piece use jazz harmony convincingly — extended chords, functional progressions, idiomatic voice leading?*

| Score | Descriptor | Concrete Anchor |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | No jazz harmony | Triads or power chords throughout. Sounds like pop or folk with a jazz label. Example: C–F–G–C repeated under a saxophone timbre. |
| **2** | Superficial jazz markers | Some 7th chords present but used as colour, not function. Progressions don't resolve logically. Example: Cmaj7–Fmaj7–Gmaj7 with no ii–V–I or tonal motion. |
| **3** | Surface-plausible but flawed | ii–V–I present and resolves, but voice leading is clunky, extensions clash, or alterations are avoided entirely. Sounds like jazz played by someone who learned chord names but not how they move. Example: Dm7–G7–Cmaj7 with parallel octaves and no chordal colour. |
| **4** | Convincing jazz harmony with minor lapses | Strong functional harmony, extensions used idiomatically, occasional awkward substitution or resolution. A working jazz musician would approve of most of it. |
| **5** | Idiomatic and inventive | Tritone substitutions, modal interchange, altered dominants used correctly and musically. Voice leading is smooth. Sounds like a competent jazz composer wrote this. Example: Db7 substituting G7 in a ii–V–I, resolving to Cmaj9 with the b9 resolving correctly. |

---

### Axis 2: Swing Feel and Microtiming

*Does the piece actually swing? This is the single hardest thing to fake — swing is a specific microtiming relationship between long and short eighth notes, plus a particular placement of accents.*

| Score | Descriptor | Concrete Anchor |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Completely straight | All eighth notes equal length. Sounds mechanical or like a metronome. No swing whatsoever. |
| **2** | Attempted swing, fails | There is rhythmic variation but it doesn't cohere into swing feel. Might sound like uneven quantisation rather than intentional lilt. |
| **3** | Weak or inconsistent swing | Swing ratio present in parts but inconsistent across the piece. Feels like a MIDI sequence with swing quantisation applied — technically there but unconvincing. Example: drums swing but melody plays straight. |
| **4** | Clear swing feel with minor inconsistencies | The piece swings recognisably; a jazz musician would tap their foot. One or two passages lose it or feel stiff. |
| **5** | Natural, idiomatic swing | The long-short ratio feels organic, not quantised. Accents fall on 2 and 4 in the drums. The melody lays back appropriately. Comparable to hearing a human jazz ensemble. Swing ratio in the range 1.5–2.0 and consistent over time. |

---

### Axis 3: Improvisational Coherence

*Does the improvised content (solos, fills, melodic development) exhibit musical logic — motivic development, call-and-response, tension and release — or is it a random sequence of jazz-sounding notes?*

| Score | Descriptor | Concrete Anchor |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Incoherent | Notes chosen seem random within a scale. No melodic logic, no phrases, no sense of direction. Sounds like a drunk person playing scales. |
| **2** | Phrases exist but don't connect | Some phrase-shaped gestures, but they don't relate to each other. No motivic development. Sounds like random licks stitched together. |
| **3** | Surface coherence, no depth | Phrases begin and end cleanly; the line sounds like jazz. But there's no motivic development — nothing comes back, nothing is developed. A statistically plausible solo. |
| **4** | Clear motivic logic | A motif introduced early recurs or develops later. Tension builds across phrases. Call-and-response between instruments. A listener would feel the line is going somewhere. |
| **5** | Excellent improvisational narrative | The solo tells a story: exposition, development, climax, resolution. Motivic transformation, rhythmic displacement, clear peak moment. Comparable to a well-constructed Parker or Rollins solo. |

---

### Axis 4: Idiomatic Jazz Vocabulary

*Are the sounds, articulations, timbres, and performance gestures recognisably jazz? This covers: swing articulation (ghost notes, slides, fall-offs), typical jazz instrumentation, correct idiomatic roles for each instrument.*

| Score | Descriptor | Concrete Anchor |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | No jazz vocabulary | Could be any genre. Generic instrumentation. No jazz-specific articulation. Example: piano solo with no comping, no walking bass, drums playing a rock beat. |
| **2** | Jazz instrumentation, wrong roles | The right instruments are present (piano, bass, drums, horn) but they behave incorrectly. Bass plays sustained notes instead of walking; piano plays full chords in the left hand instead of voicing. |
| **3** | Mostly correct roles, vocabulary thin | Instruments in correct roles, but jazz-specific articulations are generic or absent. Sounds like jazz played by classical musicians — correct but sterile. |
| **4** | Strong vocabulary with occasional lapses | Ghost notes, fall-offs, ride cymbal pattern, walking bass all present. One instrument or passage is out of idiom. |
| **5** | Fully idiomatic | Every instrument sounds like it was played by a jazz musician. Comping is sparse and reactive. Bass walks with correct chord tones on beats 1 and 3. Drummer plays the ride cymbal pattern and drops bombs. Horns use jazz articulation (shakes, bends, vibrato). |

---

### Axis 5: Ensemble Interaction

*Do the instruments respond to each other, or are they playing their own parts independently without listening? Real jazz is a conversation.*

| Score | Descriptor | Concrete Anchor |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | No interaction | Each instrument plays a fixed part with no relation to the others. Could have been recorded in separate rooms from different songs. |
| **2** | Minimal awareness | Parts are compatible but independent. Occasional rhythmic alignment by coincidence. |
| **3** | Functional ensemble | Parts fit together competently. No obvious clashes. But the instruments don't respond to each other — piano comps the same way regardless of what the soloist does. |
| **4** | Reactive playing evident | Comping changes density around the soloist's phrases. Drums set up arrivals or respond to climaxes. Not consistent but clearly present. |
| **5** | Genuine conversation | Drummer drops a bomb under a high phrase. Pianist lays out when the soloist is busy. Call-and-response between instruments. Fills in the gaps, not over the melody. Comparable to listening to a real jazz trio react in the moment. |

---

### Axis 6: Formal Structure

*Does the piece have a recognisable jazz form — head/solos/head, 12-bar blues cycles, AABA — or is it formless?*

| Score | Descriptor | Concrete Anchor |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | No discernible form | Music continues without any section divisions, repetition, or structural logic. No sense of where the piece is going. |
| **2** | Some section contrast, no jazz form | Verse/chorus-like contrast but not a jazz form. Doesn't cycle. No clear head or out-head. |
| **3** | Approximate jazz form | Something like AABA or 12-bar blues is audible but doesn't execute cleanly — chord changes happen in the wrong bar, sections are inconsistent lengths. |
| **4** | Correct form with minor inconsistencies | Recognisable jazz form that mostly works. Head stated, solos over the changes, head returns. One section miscounted or slightly misaligned. |
| **5** | Idiomatic form executed cleanly | Distinct head, improvised choruses over correct changes (correct bar count), out-head, possibly a tag or ritardando ending. A jazz musician would recognise the form immediately and be able to sit in. |

## 2. Generation Protocol

1. Use the exact prompt text from [PROMPTS.md](PROMPTS.md) without modification.
2. Accept the **first output only**. No regeneration, no cherry-picking.
3. Record: model name and version, date generated, exact prompt used, output duration.
4. Save audio files to `pieces/` as `[model]_[prompt_id]_raw.[ext]` before blinding.
5. AIVA: export MIDI and use as the analysis file. Note in scores.csv.

---

## 3. Blind Rating Procedure

2. Rater 1 (primary author) scores all 30 pieces cold, in one sitting per session where possible.
3. Share the blinded files with rater 2 with no discussion of the prompts or models until after scoring.
4. Both raters complete scoring independently before comparing notes.
5. Reconcile divergent scores (difference ≥ 2 on any axis) through discussion; document the outcome.

---

## 4. Inter-Rater Reliability

- Target: Cohen's kappa ≥ 0.6 (substantial agreement) on the total score and on each individual axis.
- If kappa < 0.6 on a given axis, the two raters discuss the rubric anchor definitions and re-rate that axis for all pieces before the axis is included in analysis.
- Kappa will be computed using `sklearn.metrics.cohen_kappa_score` on rounded total scores.

---

## 5. Quantitative Metrics

### 5.1 Swing Ratio (Audio Files)
- Implemented in `analysis/swing_ratio.py`
- Method: onset detection (librosa), pair consecutive onsets within an eighth-note window at detected tempo, compute ratio of long to short duration per pair.
- Reported: mean, standard deviation, and time-series plot.
- Reference values: 1.0 = straight; 1.5 = medium swing; 2.0 = hard swing (triplet feel).
- Limitation: onset detection is noisy on polyphonic audio; swing ratio is most meaningful for melodic lines. Treat as indicative, not definitive.

### 5.2 Chord Vocabulary (Audio + MIDI)
- Implemented in `analysis/chord_detection.py` (per-piece) and `analysis/harmonic_analysis.py` (batch stats)
- Counts: triads, dominant 7ths, major 7ths, minor 7ths, half-diminished, diminished 7ths, and uncertain segments.
- ii–V–I detection: sliding window over chord sequence using root-interval pattern matching.
- Key detection: Krumhansl-Kessler profiles applied to mean chroma vector (librosa chroma CQT).
- Pitch-class distribution: cosine similarity against jazz and pop reference distributions.

### 5.3 Self-Similarity Matrix
- To be computed on the chroma feature (librosa) to assess structural repetition and motivic recurrence.
- High diagonal blocks = strong sectional structure; diagonal stripes = motivic development.

### 5.4 Key / Tonality Detection
- music21 key analysis on MIDI.
- librosa chroma + key estimation on audio.
- Note deviations from the prompt's specified key as a separate observation.
